## Problem Statement

Thai Meet is a Thailand-focused Android/iOS dating app for Thai local women and foreign tourists who want to move from in-app chat to LINE or Facebook without awkward workarounds. In existing products, ThaiFriendly may hide LINE IDs sent as text, while Tinder/Bumble make external contact sharing feel policy-risky or socially uncomfortable.

The first product thesis is not "make contact sharing easy for everyone." The sharper thesis is: Thai local women should be able to start fresh with a new Public Meet ID and share LINE/Facebook only when they explicitly choose, using a controlled Contact Card instead of leaking raw IDs in chat text.

## Demand Evidence

Current evidence from the session:

- ThaiFriendly: LINE ID sent as plain chat text can be hidden, forcing users into workaround behavior.
- Tinder/Bumble: users may feel external contact sharing is policy-risky or unsafe.
- Founder hypothesis: Bangkok/Pattaya Thai local women and foreign tourists need a dating flow that makes LINE/Facebook transition explicit, fast, and controlled.

Evidence still missing:

- No specific Thai local woman has been named yet.
- No interview quotes, conversion numbers, waitlist deposits, community posts, or review excerpts were provided in this session.
- The main risk is that the pain is real for some users, but not strong enough to make Thai local women join a new app first.

## Status Quo

Users currently work around the problem by:

- Typing LINE IDs in ways that avoid hiding or moderation.
- Delaying LINE/Facebook exchange until trust is built inside the app.
- Moving to other social channels manually after multiple chat messages.
- Staying on incumbent apps despite poor contact-sharing UX because the user pool is already there.

The workaround cost is friction, uncertainty, and lower trust: tourists want fast coordination during a short trip, while Thai local women need control over when an external contact becomes visible.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

Initial target user:

- Thai local women in Bangkok and Pattaya, especially users who receive many messages or want separation between dating identity and private LINE/Facebook identity.

Secondary user:

- Foreign tourists from the US, Europe, China, Korea, and Japan, ages 20-50, visiting Thailand and wanting a simple way to connect with Thai local users.

Narrowest wedge:

- Public Meet ID + controlled LINE/Facebook Contact Card sharing.

The first wedge message:

> Start fresh with a new Public Meet ID. Share LINE/Facebook only when you choose. No hidden LINE ID text, no awkward workaround.

## Constraints

- Android/iOS only; no consumer web app for MVP.
- Flutter app, NestJS backend, PostgreSQL/Prisma, Redis, object storage, FCM, and AdMob remain the intended stack.
- The user selected Approach B: a PRD-scaled full MVP, so the first build includes more than the contact-card core.
- Scope must still be disciplined: advanced AI recommendation, live streaming, voice/video calling, strict identity verification, full premium subscription UX, and consumer web are excluded.
- Safety must be low-friction but real: block/report, contact exchange logs, internal userId continuity, and admin review are required.

## Premises

1. Thai local women are the supply side to seed first; without them, tourist demand will churn quickly.
2. "Easy contact sharing" must be framed as "controlled contact sharing" for Thai local women.
3. Public Meet ID regeneration is not a gimmick; it is the trust and reset mechanism that makes external contact sharing less scary.
4. A PRD-scaled full MVP can be justified only if it preserves learning gates and does not turn every PRD feature into a P0 launch blocker.
5. Advertising should be included in the full MVP, but revenue optimization should not outrank activation and contact-sharing validation.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Contact Card Validation MVP

Summary: Build only Public ID, minimum profile, Discover, chat, and LINE/Facebook Contact Card sharing.

Effort: M

Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Fastest path to testing the core wedge.
- Keeps engineering focus on the differentiated behavior.
- Makes failure easier to diagnose.

Cons:

- May feel too thin compared with incumbent dating apps.
- Does not validate ad monetization, broader activity loops, or admin operations.
- Could under-serve early users who expect a complete dating app.

### Approach B: PRD Scaled Full MVP

Summary: Build the PRD as a narrowed but launchable app: auth, Public ID, profiles/photos, Discover/search, chat, LINE/Facebook Contact Cards, basic ad monetization, 5-language UI foundation, low-friction safety, admin MVP, and analytics.

Effort: XL

Risk: High

Pros:

- Closer to a real app users can keep using after first contact exchange.
- Includes operational tools needed for safety, ads, moderation, and learning.
- Supports the founder's chosen direction without making the MVP feel like a throwaway prototype.

Cons:

- Higher chance of overbuilding before demand proof.
- More moving parts make launch delays likely.
- If activation fails, it may be hard to know whether the issue is supply, UX, trust, ads, language, or distribution.

### Approach C: Manual Market Test Before App Build

Summary: Recruit a small group of Thai local women and tourists and manually test the promise: fresh identity plus controlled LINE/Facebook sharing.

Effort: S

Risk: Low

Pros:

- Cheapest way to validate messaging and user fear.
- Could reveal which safety controls matter before coding.
- Useful for collecting exact onboarding copy and screenshots.

Cons:

- Does not produce a shippable app.
- Manual operations can distort behavior.
- Founder selected a more product-forward approach.

## Recommended Approach

Proceed with Approach B, but treat it as a staged full MVP rather than a flat all-at-once build.

The product should ship as a coherent dating app, but the internal priority order must protect the wedge:

1. Public ID + ID image + profile foundation.
2. Discover/search + profile detail.
3. Chat + LINE/Facebook Contact Card.
4. Report/block + admin visibility for user, Public ID, reports, and contact exchanges.
5. Analytics for activation, first message, contact share, and Public ID regeneration.
6. Ads and rewards after the core contact-sharing loop is stable.
7. Full polish for 5 language packs before public launch, but development can start with Korean/English plus key architecture for all five.

Approach B is approved by the founder in this session, with one condition: it must keep the Thai local women's trust proposition at the center.

### Launch Gates

Approach B remains the approved MVP scope, but it must not be executed as one undifferentiated launch block. Ship and validate it through explicit gates:

**Gate 0: Trust Loop**

- Public Meet ID creation and active identity selection.
- Minimum profile and generated ID image fallback.
- Discover grid/list enough to find real people.
- Chat room creation and text messaging.
- LINE Contact Card sharing from chat.
- Report/block for profile, chat, and contact card.
- Core analytics: onboarding complete, profile impression, first message, LINE share click, LINE card sent, report, block, Public ID generated.

Gate 0 success means Thai local women can safely try the core promise: fresh public identity plus controlled LINE sharing.

Gate 0 UX information architecture:

The Gate 0 app experience must be organized around the Trust Loop, not around a generic dating-app feature list. The first successful session should move a user from a fresh public identity to a real profile discovery and, when trust is sufficient, to a controlled LINE Contact Card.

Primary Trust Loop:

```text
Login / Social Auth
  -> Public ID Setup
  -> Minimal Profile
  -> Discover
  -> Profile Detail
  -> Chat
  -> LINE Contact Card
  -> Report / Block / Revoke safety paths
```


Gate 0 navigation implementation decision:

- Gate 0 uses the reduced Trust Loop navigation: `Discover`, `Chats`, `My ID`, and `Safety`.
- PRD v3.0's broader tab set (`Discover`, `Swipe`, `Chat`, `Activity`, `Profile`) is treated as Gate 1+ scope, not Gate 0 navigation.
- `Swipe` must not ship as a Gate 0 top-level tab. If a swipe/hot experiment is needed later, place it behind a feature flag and re-review the IA.
- `Activity` should be deferred or folded into later Gate 1 surfaces; Gate 0 safety and contact-exchange history live under `Safety`.
- `Profile` should not compete with `My ID` in Gate 0. Profile editing, Public ID history, LINE setup, and language entry points live under `My ID`.
- Route tests must assert that Gate 0 users can reach `Discover`, `Chats`, `My ID`, and `Safety` from the app shell, and that `Swipe`, `Activity`, and standalone `Profile` tabs are not visible while Gate 0 flags are active.
- Do not make the entire bottom navigation remotely configurable in Gate 0. Feature flags can hide provider/reward/ads behavior, but the app shell should remain stable for QA and onboarding.

Thai local woman Trust Storyboard:

The Gate 0 emotional design target is confidence through control. A Thai local woman should understand within five seconds that Thai Meet is not asking her to expose her private LINE identity by default. Within five minutes, she should have seen a fresh Public Meet ID, browsed people, and understood that LINE sharing is an explicit card action she can report, block, or revoke.

| Step | User does | User feels | UI support |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Opens app and logs in | Uses social login and enters onboarding. | Curious but cautious; wants to know what will be exposed. | Login copy states that public identity and private contact details are separate. |
| 2. Receives Public Meet ID | Sees generated Public ID and ID image before Discover. | Relief: "I can start fresh without showing my real LINE yet." | Public ID card is the visual anchor, with short copy explaining that internal safety history still stays protected. |
| 3. Completes minimum profile | Adds only required dating profile fields. | Momentum; the app is not making her over-explain herself. | Required fields are few, visible, and saved progressively; optional photos can wait. |
| 4. Browses Discover | Looks at profiles in Bangkok or Pattaya. | In control; she can scan without commitment. | Active Public ID remains visible in the header; filters are in a bottom sheet, not dominant chrome. |
| 5. Opens profile and starts chat | Opens a profile and creates a room without match friction. | Low pressure; the next step is obvious. | Start Chat is primary; Report and Block are visible but secondary. |
| 6. Considers LINE sharing | Taps LINE Contact Card from Chat Detail. | Alert but not trapped; this is the sensitive moment. | First-share confirmation states exactly what will be shared and that Thai Meet cannot control outside-app conversation. |
| 7. Sends Contact Card | Confirms and sends controlled card. | Agency; she chose to share, instead of being tricked by raw text. | Card shows provider, recipient, Open/Copy states for receiver, and Report/Block/Revoke actions. |
| 8. Handles bad behavior | Reports, blocks, or revokes a card after a bad interaction. | Protected; reset does not feel like starting from zero. | Safety actions are one tap from Profile Detail, Chat Detail, and each Contact Card. |
| 9. Generates a new Public ID | Uses My ID to start fresh later. | Renewal without panic; old activity is contained. | New ID flow explains that old chats are archived and user-level blocks/reports still apply. |

Time horizon requirements:

- First 5 seconds: user sees Thai Meet, active Public ID separation, and no private contact leakage.
- First 5 minutes: user reaches Discover, understands direct chat, and sees that LINE sharing is a controlled Contact Card.
- Long-term trust: user can regenerate Public ID without bypassing safety history, losing audit continuity, or mixing old identity chats into the new identity surface.

Gate 0 app UI anti-slop rules:

Thai Meet is an app UI, not a marketing site. The interface should feel calm, mobile-native, and trust-focused. Avoid generic dating-app visuals that make Public ID and Contact Card look like decorative features instead of safety and control mechanisms.

Hard avoid:

- Do not build the first screen as a generic dating-card feed with no Public ID context.
- Do not use a large marketing hero, feature grid, or centered promotional copy inside the app shell.
- Do not make every surface a rounded card; use cards only for actual profile cards, Public ID cards, Contact Cards, and list rows that behave like selectable objects.
- Do not make ads or rewarded actions visually louder than chat, LINE sharing, report, block, or Public ID controls.
- Do not use generic copy such as "connect instantly", "unlock meaningful connections", or "all-in-one dating experience."
- Do not hide safety actions inside a deep overflow menu when the user is looking at a profile, chat, or Contact Card.
- Do not make Contact Card look like a normal chat bubble; it must visibly differ because it carries external-contact permission.

Concrete UI direction:

- Public ID is the primary identity anchor. Show the active Public ID chip/card in onboarding, Discover header, Chat Detail context, and My ID.
- Discover should be dense and scannable: profile photo or ID image, city, age, short status, and Start Chat. Avoid decorative profile summaries that push actions below the fold.
- Profile Detail should prioritize identity, city/travel context, Start Chat, and safety actions before long biography content.
- Chat Detail should keep text messaging simple and make the LINE Contact Card action visible, but not visually coercive.
- Contact Card should read like a permission object: provider, sender Public ID, receiver, created time/status, Open/Copy actions, and Report/Block/Revoke paths.
- Rewarded Ad CTAs should be utility prompts, not emotional pressure. They can unlock extra actions only after the user hits a limit or enters a clearly optional reward surface.
- Safety surfaces should be plain, readable, and action-oriented. Avoid dramatic warning visuals unless there is an actual high-risk event.

Litmus checks before implementation:

- Can a user identify their current Public ID within three seconds?
- Can a user tell whether LINE has or has not been shared yet?
- Can a user report or block from any profile, chat, or Contact Card surface?
- Can the app still feel trustworthy if all shadows, gradients, and decorative illustrations are removed?
- Does every card earn its existence as an interactive object or state container?

Public ID image principles:

- Template-based only for MVP; do not generate AI faces.
- Must include Thai Meet brand mark, Public ID, nickname or handle, color pattern, and optional symbol.
- Must never resemble a government ID, age verification document, or official credential.
- Must work as a fallback profile image before user photo upload.
- Gate 0 visual direction is a non-ID badge template, not a realistic identity document.
- The first Figma/design pass should create six editable template directions:
  - Soft badge: friendly rounded badge with Public ID and simple symbol.
  - City card: Bangkok/Pattaya city cue without tourist-poster clutter.
  - Pattern card: abstract color pattern plus handle, optimized for photo fallback.
  - Minimal handle: plain typographic card for users who want low visual noise.
  - Travel-pass inspired: lightly references travel, but must not look official or government-issued.
  - Photo fallback frame: neutral frame used when the user later adds a real profile photo.
- Every template must pass the "not official ID" test: no document numbers, signatures, seals, hologram effects, flags as authority markers, or identity-verification wording.

Copy principles:

- Use control-first language: "Share LINE when you choose", not "connect faster."
- Safety copy should be short and direct; do not write long warnings users will skip.
- Provider copy must state exactly what is shared and when it leaves Thai Meet.
- Reward copy must be transactional and optional; no pressure language.

## Database Constraints And Index Matrix

Gate 0 schema work must include constraints and indexes up front. These are part of correctness, not later optimization.

Recommended constraints and indexes:

```text
UserIdentity:
  unique(provider, providerUserId)
  index(userId)

PublicIdentity:
  unique(publicId)
  unique(handle) where handle is not null
  index(userId, status)
  index(status, createdAt)

ExternalContact:
  unique(userId, provider) where isActive=true
  index(userId, provider)
  index(publicIdentityId)

ChatRoomParticipant:
  unique(roomId, userId, publicIdentityIdAtCreation)
  index(userId, publicIdentityIdAtCreation)
  index(roomId)

ChatMessage:
  index(roomId, createdAt)
  index(senderUserId, createdAt)
  index(senderPublicIdentityId)
  ensure contact-card messages reference contactExchangeId and do not duplicate raw contact payload

ContactExchange:
  index(roomId, createdAt)
  index(senderUserId, receiverUserId)
  index(contactId)
  index(status, createdAt)

Block:
  unique(blockerUserId, blockedUserId)
  index(blockedUserId)

Report:
  index(reportedUserId, createdAt)
  index(reportedPublicIdentityId, createdAt)
  index(reporterUserId, createdAt)

RewardLedger:
  unique(idempotencyKey) where idempotencyKey is not null
  index(userId, type, expiresAt)
  index(publicIdentityId)

RewardConsumption:
  index(rewardLedgerId)
  index(actionType, actionId)

AuditEvent:
  index(actorUserId, createdAt)
  index(targetUserId, createdAt)
  index(eventType, createdAt)
```

Transaction boundaries:

- Social login must create or resolve `User`, `UserIdentity`, initial `PublicIdentity`, and `activePublicIdentityId` atomically.
- Public ID activation must atomically update `User.activePublicIdentityId` and identity status rules.
- Contact sharing must atomically create `ContactExchange` and the corresponding `ChatMessage`.
- Reward grant and reward consumption must be transactional and idempotent.
- Report/block actions must write audit events in the same logical operation.
